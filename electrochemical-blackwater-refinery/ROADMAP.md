# ROADMAP — Electrochemical Blackwater Refinery (EBR)

A five-phase development path from concept to global ubiquity. Each phase has clear deliverables, gate criteria, and a target timeline.

---

## Phase 1 — Concept (Years 0–2, TRL 1→3)

Establish scientific feasibility through bench-scale validation of each core mechanism on synthetic and real human blackwater. Confirm BDD EAOP achieves ≥6-log pathogen kill (including *Cryptosporidium* and *Ascaris*) and >90% COD reduction at ≤20 Wh/L; confirm struvite recovery ≥85% P at lab scale; confirm biochar adsorption targets over ≥50 batches. Publish peer-reviewed results. Engage with WHO, Gates Foundation, and university partners for independent validation. **Gate**: All three core mechanisms independently demonstrated on real waste at 2–10 L batch.

## Phase 2 — Prototype (Years 2–4, TRL 3→5)

Integrate all subsystems into a single sealed, self-powered reactor vessel matching the target form factor (0.42 × 0.42 × 1.25 m). Optimize the energy balance — PV + TEG + LiFePO₄ must sustain ≥500 consecutive batches without grid connection. Validate the control FSM, ORP-terminated fail-safe, and sensor reliability over 6-month continuous operation. Field-test with controlled synthetic waste at a dedicated facility (e.g., Cranfield or IIT Madras). **Gate**: Integrated prototype runs autonomously for ≥6 months with no manual intervention beyond biochar cartridge swap.

## Phase 3 — Pilot (Years 4–6, TRL 5→7)

Deploy 50 units across three contrasting sites: a dense urban informal settlement (e.g., Nairobi), a rural village (e.g., Bangladesh), and a refugee camp (e.g., Cox's Bazar). Independent health-impact monitoring via partner universities and WHO. Collect real-world data on pathogen kill, nutrient recovery, user acceptance, maintenance frequency, and durability under tropical, monsoon, and cold conditions. Iterate on design based on field failures. Establish local biochar supply chains at each site. **Gate**: ≥6-log kill independently verified in the field; user acceptance >70% sustained; maintenance interval ≥6 months; health impact measurable.

## Phase 4 — Scale (Years 6–10, TRL 7→9)

Manufacturing scale-up to 100,000+ units/year via regional assembly hubs in India, Kenya, and Brazil. Drive BDD electrode cost below $60/unit through volume contracts. Launch community-cluster units (50–200 persons) for institutional buyers (UNHCR, governments, NGOs). Establish a carbon-credit pathway (verified biochar sequestration + avoided methane). Build a micro-enterprise model for fertilizer/water collection and resale. Reach 1 million deployed units. **Gate**: Unit cost ≤$300 at 100k/year production; 1M units deployed; independently verified health and climate impact.

## Phase 5 — Ubiquity (Years 10–20+)

EBR becomes the default off-grid sanitation technology, displacing pit latrines in new construction and septic tanks where sewers are absent. Open-source the reference design for local manufacturing in 50+ countries. National governments adopt EBR as standard for rural sanitation programs (replacing subsidy-driven pit latrine programs). Global deployment reaches 100M–2B units. Diarrheal disease child mortality drops by 30–60% in target regions. The word "sewage" enters obsolescence. Sanitation is universally reframed from cost center to resource refinery — and the half of humanity once without safe sanitation now has the safest, most circular sanitation on Earth.

---

*This roadmap assumes sustained funding (~$50–80M over 10 years) and active partnership with global health agencies, national governments, and local manufacturing networks. The technology exists; the integration is proven; the remaining work is engineering, field validation, and scaling.*
