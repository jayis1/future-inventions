# Mycoremediation Living Filter

## Problem

**Persistent organic pollutants — particularly PFAS ("forever chemicals") — contaminate the drinking water of billions of people worldwide.**

- PFAS are found in **45%+ of US tap water** and virtually all surface water globally
- The C–F bond in PFAS is one of the strongest in organic chemistry (~485 kJ/mol), making these compounds resist natural degradation for millennia
- Current remediation methods — activated carbon adsorption, ion exchange resins, and high-temperature incineration — **merely transfer pollutants** rather than destroying them, at costs of **$3–10/m³** treated water
- Incineration of PFAS requires >1,400°C and risks generating toxic fluorinated byproducts
- An estimated **4.7 billion people** drink water with detectable levels of at least one persistent organic pollutant (PFAS, pharmaceutical residues, organochlorine pesticides)
- Agricultural runoff, industrial discharge, and landfill leachate continuously add to the burden
- Regulatory limits are tightening worldwide (US EPA: 4 ppt for PFOA/PFOS) but affordable destruction technologies don't exist yet

## Solution

The **Mycoremediation Living Filter (MLF)** is a modular bioreactor that **enzymatically destroys** persistent organic pollutants — converting PFAS into harmless fluoride ions, CO₂, and water — using an engineered consortium of white-rot fungi and electrogenic bacteria, hosted within a self-regenerating mycelial sponge matrix.

Unlike conventional approaches that capture-and-concentrate pollutants, the MLF **mineralizes them in place**, eliminating the secondary waste stream problem entirely. The system is powered by a low-voltage bioelectrochemical boost (0.5–1.2 V) that enhances enzymatic C–F bond cleavage rates by 10–50×, making biodegradation kinetically competitive with chemical oxidation.

### How It Works

1. **Contaminated water** flows through the mycelial sponge matrix at a controlled hydraulic residence time (~4–8 hours for >99% PFAS destruction)
2. **Engineered white-rot fungi** (*Phanerochaete chrysosporium* and *Pleurotus ostreatus*) express evolved fluorinated compound dehalogenase enzymes that catalyze C–F bond cleavage via reductive defluorination
3. **Electrogenic bacteria** (*Geobacter sulfurreducens*, *Shewanella oneidensis*) provide reducing equivalents (electrons) to sustain the enzymatic reaction, powered by a minimal applied voltage
4. **The mycelial matrix** serves triple duty: structural scaffold ensuring uniform flow distribution, mass-transfer optimizer (fungal hyphae create nano-scale channels), and self-regenerating biocatalytic surface
5. **Defluorination products** — fluoride ions (F⁻), short-chain carboxylic acids, CO₂, and H₂O — exit harmlessly; fluoride is captured by a downstream calcium hydroxide trap
6. **The living system self-repairs**: mycelium regrows to fill voids, bacteria replicate, and the consortium adapts its enzyme expression profile to match incoming pollutant mixtures via quorum-sensing regulation

## Key Innovation

**The convergence of three advances makes this feasible for the first time:**

1. **Evolved perfluoroalkyl defluorinases** — Directed evolution (using phage-assisted continuous evolution, PACE) has produced enzyme variants capable of cleaving C–F bonds with catalytic efficiencies (k_cat/K_M) 1,000× higher than naturally occurring fluorinated-compound degrading enzymes. These are expressed from synthetic operons integrated into the fungal genome.

2. **Bioelectrochemical rate enhancement** — The thermodynamic barrier to C–F bond reduction (E° ≈ –2.0 V vs SHE for direct electrochemical reduction) is circumvented by enzymatic catalysis, while a low applied voltage (0.5–1.2 V) drives electron flow through the Geobacter/Shewanella biofilm to regenerate the reduced enzyme active site. This synergy achieves **10–50× rate acceleration** over purely biological degradation.

3. **Self-regenerating mycelial bioreactor architecture** — The fungal mycelium forms a living, porous 3D matrix (90–95% porosity, ~1 mm channel diameter) that provides enormous catalytic surface area (~500 m²/m³ reactor volume), uniform flow distribution, and — critically — **continuous self-repair**. Damaged or spent hyphae are replaced by new growth, eliminating the need for media replacement that plagues conventional biofilters.

## Impact

- **2B+ people** could gain access to PFAS-free drinking water through municipal deployment
- **Eliminates the secondary waste problem** — no concentrated PFAS brine or spent carbon to incinerate
- **500× reduction in energy** vs. electrochemical oxidation approaches
- **Applicable to diverse pollutants**: PFAS, pharmaceuticals, pesticides, industrial solvents — the consortium's enzyme expression adapts via quorum sensing
- **Scalable from household (10 L/day) to municipal (100,000 m³/day)**
- **Carbon-negative operation**: fungal metabolism fixes more CO₂ into biomass than the system emits
- **Democratizes clean water**: low capital and operating costs make this viable for low-income communities and developing nations
- **Environmental justice**: disproportionately benefits communities near military bases, industrial sites, and airports where PFAS contamination is most severe
