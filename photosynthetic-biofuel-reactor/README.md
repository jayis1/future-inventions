# Photosynthetic Biofuel Reactor

## Problem

Biofuels compete with food production for land. Corn ethanol uses 40% of US corn crop. Algae biofuel is promising but requires massive open ponds, contamination control, and expensive harvesting. Fossil fuel dependence continues.

## Solution

A closed photobioreactor using engineered cyanobacteria (Synechococcus elongatus) with a synthetic pathway that directly excretes liquid hydrocarbon (farnesene) into the growth medium. No harvesting, no extraction — the fuel separates by density and is continuously siphoned off.

## Key Innovation

- **Engineered cyanobacteria** — synthetic biology inserts farnesene synthase pathway, bacteria secrete drop-in diesel replacement
- **Continuous flow flat-panel reactor** — 10x surface-to-volume ratio vs open ponds, prevents contamination
- **Self-separating product** — farnesene (C₁₅H₂₄) is immiscible with water, floats to top, skimmed off
- **CO₂ feed from industrial capture** — waste CO₂ becomes fuel, closing the carbon cycle
- **No arable land needed** — desert, rooftop, ocean-floating deployment

## Target Cost

$1.20/gallon diesel equivalent (vs $3.30 petroleum diesel)

## Impact
- Carbon-neutral fuel — CO₂ captured = CO₂ emitted when burned
- Zero competition with food production
- 10x land efficiency vs corn ethanol per unit energy
- Works on marginal/degraded land — restores nothing, takes nothing
- Drop-in replacement for existing diesel engines and infrastructure
