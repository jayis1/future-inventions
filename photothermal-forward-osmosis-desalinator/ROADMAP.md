# Roadmap — Photothermal Forward-Osmosis Desalinator

> From lab bench to global ubiquity: five phases over 15 years.

---

## Phase 1 — Concept (Years 0–2)

Validate the core hypothesis: that a thermo-responsive ionic hydrogel can serve simultaneously as an FO draw agent (generating ≥40 bar osmotic pressure) and as a self-regenerating medium (releasing ≥70% absorbed water via solar-triggered LCST phase transition). Conduct laboratory experiments on poly(NIPAm-co-SA) copolymer formulations varying comonomer ratio (0–30 mol% SA), crosslinker density (1–5 mol% MBAAm), and photothermal filler loading (0–5 wt% carbon black / polydopamine). Characterize swelling ratios, osmotic pressure via stopped-flow osmometry, LCST via DSC and turbidimetry, photothermal heating rates under simulated solar irradiation, and cycle durability over 50+ thermal swings. Publish peer-reviewed results securing the fundamental science and identifying optimal formulations.

## Phase 2 — Prototype (Years 2–5)

Build the first integrated bench-top PFOD unit: a 0.5 m² FO membrane paired with 2 kg of optimized hydrogel in a manually operated two-chamber cell with a borosilicate regeneration cover and SiO₂/PDMS radiative cooling surface. Demonstrate ≥3 L/kg dry gel water absorption per cycle and ≥70% recovery under 1 sun in 30 minutes. Achieve ≥100 cumulative cycles at ≥80% capacity retention. Optimize membrane-hydrogel interface to minimize internal concentration polarization (ICP), the dominant flux-limiting phenomenon in FO. Begin NSF/ANSI 61 materials review and toxicological leachate testing. File foundational patents on the gel formulation and system architecture.

## Phase 3 — Pilot (Years 5–8)

Deploy 10 community-scale pilot modules (5 m³/day rated) at diverse field sites: a SIDS nation (Maldives or Kiribati), an arid coastal region (Gaza or Yemen via NGO partners), and a disaster-response depot (IFRC stockpile). Run 12-month unattended field trials collecting daily yield, water quality, gel degradation, and operational reliability data. Establish local technician training programs (2-hour certification curriculum). Refine gel formulation for extended cycle life (≥500 cycles). Launch regulatory engagement for WHO endorsement and national drinking water certification in 3 target countries. Achieve LCOE of ≤$0.40/m³ demonstrated in the field.

## Phase 4 — Scale (Years 8–12)

Transition to mass manufacturing: establish gel-production facility (≥50 tonnes/year), FO membrane sourcing partnership, and module assembly line (≥10,000 units/year unit cost ≤$1,500 for 5 m³/day module). Deploy 100,000+ modules across SIDS, Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Middle East via development bank financing (World Bank, ADB, AfDB) and humanitarian procurement (UNICEF, Oxfam). Build fleet-management telemetry platform (LoRaWAN dashboard) covering ≥90% uptime. Train 5,000+ local technicians across 50+ countries. Achieve verified water access for 5+ million people and publish independent impact assessments.

## Phase 5 — Ubiquity (Years 12–15+)

PFOD modules become standard infrastructure alongside solar panels and rainwater tanks — sold in hardware stores, subsidized by ministries of water, included in disaster preparedness kits. Open-source gel formulations enable regional manufacturing in 20+ countries, reducing logistics costs and building local technical capacity. Global deployment reaches 500M+ direct beneficiaries. Desalination's share of global electricity demand falls by 50%+. The term "water scarcity" recedes from public discourse for coastal and brackish-aquifer populations. PFOD joins solar lighting, mobile phones, and mosquito nets as a transformative essential technology — universally accessible, locally owned, and powered by nothing but the sun.
